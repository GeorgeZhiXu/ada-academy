The Debate Guidebook

Everything you need to debate like a pro.

Chapters

1. What is Debate? 2. POE Structure 3. Evidence & Examples 4. Clash & Rebuttal 5. Weighing & Impact 6. Advanced Techniques 7. Common Mistakes

1. What is Debate?

Debate is a structured argument between two sides about a topic. One side argues FOR (proposition) and the other argues AGAINST (opposition). The goal is not to shout louder — it's to make the most logical, well-supported, and persuasive case.

Key principle: A good debater can argue either side convincingly. It's about skill, not personal belief.

Debates have rounds. In the early rounds (constructive speeches), you present your arguments. In the later rounds (rebuttals), you respond to your opponent and summarize why your side wins. You never introduce new arguments in rebuttal rounds.

2. The POE Structure

Every argument you make should follow POE:

P

Point

State your argument in one clear sentence.

"My argument is that school uniforms save families money."
O

Observation / Explanation

Explain why your point is true. What's the logic behind it?

"This is because parents no longer need to buy expensive trendy clothes for their kids to fit in at school."
E

Evidence / Example

Give a concrete fact, statistic, or real-world example.

"For example, a study in the Long Beach school district found families saved an average of $150 per year after uniforms were introduced."
Intermediate tip: Add a fourth step — Impact. Explain why your point matters in the big picture: "This is significant because it reduces financial stress on low-income families."

3. Evidence & Examples

Arguments without evidence are just opinions. Strong debaters back up every claim.

Strong evidence

  • Statistics and data ("Studies show 73% of...")
  • Real-world examples ("In Japan, this policy led to...")
  • Expert opinions ("According to Dr. Smith...")
  • Historical precedent ("When this was tried in 2015...")

Weak evidence

  • "Everyone knows..." (no source)
  • "I feel like..." (personal opinion)
  • "My friend said..." (anecdotal)
  • "It's obvious that..." (assertion without proof)

4. Clash & Rebuttal

Clash means directly engaging with your opponent's arguments — not just repeating your own. This is what separates beginners from skilled debaters.

1

Identify their claim

"My opponent argued that homework improves test scores."
2

Attack the reasoning

"However, this assumes that more practice always means better results. In reality, studies show diminishing returns after 90 minutes of homework."
3

Provide your counter-evidence

"In fact, Finland assigns almost no homework and consistently ranks among the top education systems globally."
Never say: "That's wrong" without explaining why. Always give a reason their argument fails.

5. Weighing & Impact

In later rounds, don't just repeat arguments — weigh them. Explain why your side's impacts matter more than your opponent's.

The weighing formula: "Even if my opponent is right about [their point], my argument about [your point] matters more because [reason: scope, severity, probability]."

Three ways to weigh:

Scope

How many people does it affect? "My argument affects millions of families, while theirs only concerns a small group."

Severity

How serious is the impact? "We're talking about people's health vs. minor inconvenience."

Probability

How likely is the outcome? "My scenario is almost certain, while theirs is speculative."

6. Advanced Techniques

Framing

Tell the judge HOW to evaluate the debate: "Judge, you should weigh real-world evidence over theoretical arguments."

Steel-Manning

Present the strongest version of your opponent's argument before taking it apart. This shows you understand their position and still win: "The best version of their case is... but even then, it fails because..."

Turning an Argument

Show that your opponent's own argument actually helps YOUR side: "They say competition is good — I agree! And that's exactly why my policy creates healthier competition."

No New Arguments in Rebuttal

In rebuttal rounds, you can only respond to existing arguments and summarize. Introducing new points is against the rules and will lose you points.

7. Common Mistakes

Don't do this

  • Making claims without evidence
  • Ignoring your opponent's arguments
  • Getting emotional or personal
  • Introducing new arguments in rebuttal
  • Repeating the same point 3 times
  • Using "everyone knows" as evidence

Do this instead

  • Back every claim with POE
  • Directly clash with their points
  • Stay calm, logical, and respectful
  • Summarize and weigh in rebuttals
  • Make 2-3 strong arguments, not 10 weak ones
  • Cite specific examples and data

FAQ

Is this free to use?

Debating Pro is free for a full year. No credit card, no hidden fees. Your data is stored locally on your device.

Does it use a real AI like ChatGPT?

The AI opponent uses a built-in response engine that adapts to your skill level. It runs entirely in your browser — no internet connection or API key needed.

How is my score calculated?

Your arguments are analyzed for clarity (structure and length), evidence (use of examples and data), persuasion (confident language), and rebuttal (engaging with the AI's points). Each category is scored out of 10.

Can I use this for school or practice?

Absolutely. Debating Pro is designed for students, debate club members, public speaking learners, and anyone who wants to sharpen their argumentation skills.

Is my data private?

All your data (settings, history, stats) is stored in your browser's local storage. Nothing is sent to any server. Clearing your browser data will reset everything.
